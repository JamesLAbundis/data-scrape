Democratic Presidential Primary

The analysis studys factors such as race, sex, income, etc., that contribute to the fraction of the vote each candidate wins. A multi-variable linear regression model was used to test these factors and predict future primaries.

How Demographics Shaped the Final Result

This graph shows the extent to which each socio-economic factor contributed to the fraction of the vote won by each candidate. The higher the R-Squared value, the stronger the relationship is between a socio-economic factor and the fraction of the vote. According to my analysis, education, race, and income are the strongest indicators of how much of the vote each candidate will win.

R-Squared values are interpreted as the following: Values closer to 1 suggest a stronger correlation (relationship) between two variables, values closer to 0 suggest a weaker correlation (relationship) between two variables.

Slope should be interpreted as the following: a negative slope suggests a negative correlation (relationship) between two variables and a positive slope will just be the opposite of this. For example a negative slope between education and the fraction of the vote for Hillary Clinton suggests that as a population's education increases, the fraction of the vote for Hillary Clinton will decrease.

Hover over each bar to display the R-Squared value and the slope, which will indicate a positive or negative correlation.

Prediction Model

After studying the correlations between the above socio-economic variables/factors and the response variable: fraction of the vote, I decided to make a multi-variable linear regression model using these factors to predict future primaries. My results are split into two categories: predictions that happened after the publishing of the data set (Late March) and primaries that have yet to happen at the time of this post. We can use the first category to test the model accuracy and study the second category to anticipate future outcomes.

Model Accuracy

I calculated the model accuracy by finding the number of primaries that the model predicted correctly over the total number of primaries that happened after the data set was published. The model was able to predict the correct winner of a primary 64.3% of the time.

It is important to note that this model represents a snapshot of the Democratic Presidential Primary. Despite being able to include several socio-economic factors in my model, there are factors that I was unable to measure such as: media coverage for each candidate, social media presence, media bias, Google searches for each candidate, and etc.

Lastly, some of the predicted win margins above are quite unrealistic. It is very rare that any candidate wins a state by a 50 or 40 perecent margin, but this should be interpreted as one candidate is predicted to handily win a state over the other.

Conclusion

My analysis in conclusion has brought up interesting trends that have even taken me by surprise. Part of my motivation to study the Democratic Presidential Primary was to find my own conclusions about why or why not certain candidates were winning or losing. The media to some extent has claimed that Sanders does better among states with large white populations and Clinton fairs better with larger women and minority populations. My analysis however disconfirms most of these claims and has found that each candidate has support from all ages and both genders. Race is a factor in determining the fraction of the vote, however the correlation is not as strong as the media claimed it to be.

It is also important to note the following: My model does not reflect several media influences that shape the outcome of the Democratic Presidential Primary. Other factors such as candidate likeability, candidate scandals, and key events during the course of a Democratic Presidential Primary can affect state primary results significantly. Despite doing my best to make an accurate model, I would not be surprised if my predictions for the June 7th super Tuesday are proven wrong. I will post a more in depth post-analysis after the 2016 Democratic Presidential Primary has finished. Stay tuned and as always, thank you for reading.
